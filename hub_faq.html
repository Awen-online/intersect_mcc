Section 1

💵 The Money — Financial Accountability Questions

The most common and most legitimate category of concern. Here is a precise accounting of what was and was not required — and why.

The Reality

The hub program had two distinct funding phases with different accountability structures — and this distinction matters a great deal.

  • Phase 1 (approx. 6 months): Funded by Cardano Development Holdings (CDH), a private organization. These were private contracts, not community treasury disbursements. The same public disclosure standard that applies to treasury grants does not — and legally cannot — be retroactively applied to private contracts.
  • Phase 2 (April – June 2025 bridge): Funded through public treasury withdrawal. These amounts are public record: North America 16,216 ADA · LATAM 23,438 ADA · Sri Lanka 18,182 ADA · Africa 18,182 ADA · Japan 12,000 ADA.
  • All activity was milestone-gated: 48 Milestone Acceptance Forms (MAFs), DocuSign-verified, were required before any payment was released. Work was verified — not taken on faith.

💬 Talking Point

"When someone says 'no accountability,' ask them which phase they mean. Phase 1 was private CDH funding under private contracts — the same standard you'd apply to any vendor relationship. Phase 2 is on-chain, publicly verifiable. And every single payment in both phases required a signed milestone form confirming deliverables were met before the money moved. That's not 'no accountability' — that's how contracted work operates."

The Reality

Japan's detailed cost breakdown is genuinely commendable. It is also worth being precise about what it represents: a self-reported Google Sheet from a hub that wound down operations and needed to account for funds being returned. The circumstance created the reporting — it was not required by the contract.

All six hubs operated under the same CDH private contract structure, which did not mandate detailed financial disclosure. Holding five hubs to a standard that was never a contract requirement — and asking them to reconstruct 2-year-old records retroactively — is not a reasonable accountability mechanism. It also is not how private contracting works in any other context.

What we do have for all hubs: activity documentation, milestone sign-offs, event records, and membership outcomes. That is the accountability record that was required.

💬 Talking Point

"Japan's reporting sets the standard we want going forward — and it's built into the 2026 framework. But let's be honest about what we're comparing: Japan reported because they were returning funds and winding down. The other hubs completed their contracts. The gap isn't evidence of wrongdoing — it's a structural omission in the original contract design. That's a solvable problem. We solved it in 2026."

The Reality

These are the Phase 2 bridge payment amounts — the treasury-funded period. The ADA amounts are publicly confirmed. What we do not have are itemized receipts for those funds, because the contracts for this period did not require them. That is a gap in contract design, and it is one the 2026 framework directly addresses.

Using Japan as a benchmark: Japan spent approximately $19,000 USD across events (55%), staff labor (26%), hub management (18%), and tools (1%). For a hub of similar scope and 3-month duration, that breakdown provides a reasonable reference range for what hub operational costs look like. It is not a perfect answer — but it is a meaningful one.

💬 Talking Point

"The 'where did it go' question deserves a real answer, and the honest answer is: the Phase 2 amounts are on-chain, the Phase 1 amounts were private contracts. For what hub operations actually cost, Japan's breakdown is the best public reference we have — events, labor, management, tools. The 2026 contracts require all of that to be reported by default. That's the structural fix."

Section 2

📊 The Numbers — Data & Metrics Questions

Concerns about reported figures, methodology, and whether the metrics can be trusted.

The Reality

Reporting was inconsistent across hubs. That is accurate. Six hubs operated in six different regions under different cultural, organizational, and operational contexts, without a mandated common reporting standard. Inconsistency in reporting does not invalidate the underlying data — it means the data requires interpretation, which the post-mortem provides.

The numbers that are cross-verifiable — membership figures, milestone completion records, contract amounts — are sourced from Intersect's own documentation, not hub self-reporting. Member counts are from Intersect's membership system. Contract amounts are from DocuSign-verified MAF documents. These are not estimates.

Inconsistent reporting is, however, precisely the gap the 2026 framework closes — standardized KPI reporting is a core requirement.

💬 Talking Point

"Reporting inconsistency is a real criticism of the 2025 program structure. It's also why the membership numbers in the post-mortem come from Intersect's own system — not hub self-reports. The numbers we're citing are verifiable. The 2026 framework standardizes reporting so this isn't a conversation we need to have again."

Section 3

✅ The Value — Was It Worth It?

The ROI question. What did the program actually produce, and how does it compare to not having hubs at all?

The Reality

The post-mortem represents the most complete picture of hub activity and outcomes ever compiled. The data we have is not perfect — but it is substantial, and it points in one consistent direction.

  • 70% of Intersect's individual members are located in hub regions. That correlation is not coincidental.
  • Japan: 533 individual members from 61 events at ~$19K total spend ≈ ~$36 per individual member acquired in direct event costs.
  • North America: 239 individual members, 225 associates, 6 enterprise members from 12 events.
  • These are not passive event attendees — these are people who made an active commitment to join and participate in Intersect's governance.

The relevant comparison is not "was every dollar optimally deployed?" It is: what is the alternative cost of acquiring committed, governance-active members through digital-only channels? Hubs create in-person trust that online channels cannot replicate at this depth.

If the concern is specifically "we need itemized expense receipts from private CDH-funded contracts" — that is a different bar, and one that cannot be retroactively applied to private contracting arrangements. No reasonable procurement standard requires it. If that is the condition for approval, it is not a realistic one.

If the concern is "we need confidence that 2026 will have the accountability mechanisms 2025 lacked" — that is a forward-looking question, and it has a concrete answer: the 2026 framework. Review the framework. The accountability structure is built in from the start.

💬 Talking Point

"The post-mortem is the answer to 'show us what happened.' It's the most complete picture we've ever had. If the concern is about 2026 accountability rather than 2025 history — that's the right question, and the framework answers it. What we should not do is treat an imperfect past as a reason to forfeit the momentum hubs created. Delaying 2026 doesn't recover 2025 data. It just loses the community relationships we built."

The Reality

This concern reflects a real tension: a transparent report that acknowledges gaps is harder to use as promotional material than a polished success narrative. But it is significantly more credible — and credibility is exactly what the hubs program needs right now.

A report that honestly documents the financial disclosure gaps, explains why they existed, and identifies what the 2026 framework changes is not ammunition for opponents. It is the response to opponents. It demonstrates that the program has been evaluated honestly, not whitewashed.

A hub opponent can always say "the report is incomplete." They cannot credibly say "the report is dishonest" — because the report says the same things they're saying, and then answers them.

💬 Talking Point

That's a much stronger position than a report that claims everything was perfect. Opponents can argue with a polished narrative. They can't argue with a self-critical assessment that then makes the case for 2026."

Section 4

🚀 The Future — Why 2026 Is Different

Every structural gap identified in 2025 has a specific remedy in the 2026 framework. Here is the direct mapping.

The Reality

Three structural differences that directly address the 2025 gaps:

  • Funding source: 2026 hubs will be funded from the community treasury, not private CDH funds. Treasury funding carries explicit public accountability requirements that private contracts do not.
  • Reporting requirements: Standardized financial reporting and KPI disclosure are built into the 2026 contracts from day one — not added retroactively.
  • Community approval: The community approves the framework, the budget, and can set the conditions. The accountability structure is not internal to Intersect — it is governed by the same community asking the questions.

The hubs program also now has something it did not have in 2025: a post-mortem. A documented record of what worked, what didn't, and what the program actually produced. That is institutional knowledge the 2026 design is built on.

💬 Talking Point

"2026 is different because the funding structure is different, the reporting requirements are different, and — critically — the community governs the approval. You don't have to trust us. You have to approve the framework. That's the accountability mechanism. Review the framework, set your conditions, and vote."

The Reality

"Mismanagement" implies behavioral failure. The gaps in 2025 were structural, not behavioral: reporting was inconsistent because reporting standards were not mandated. Financial disclosure was uneven because the contracts did not require it uniformly. These are contract design problems, not character problems.

Evidence of actual operational delivery: 35 original contracts and 13 interim contracts completed with milestone sign-offs. 167 events run across six regions. 2,395 total Intersect members in hub regions. Six regions maintained active presence across a 14-month program period. That is not mismanagement — that is delivery under an imperfect accountability structure.

The people who ran 2025 hubs also built the community relationships, the local credibility, and the institutional knowledge that a new program needs. Replacing them to signal accountability theater would cost more than it gains.

💬 Talking Point

"Point to a specific operational failure. What was asked for and not delivered? The milestone forms exist. The events happened. The members joined. The gaps were in reporting requirements that weren't written into the contracts — that's a design problem, not an execution problem. 2026 fixes the design."

What Changes in 2026
  • Treasury funding: On-chain, publicly verifiable from day one — not private CDH contracts.
  • Mandatory financial reporting: Standardized expense reporting built into every hub contract, not optional.
  • Standardized KPIs: Common metrics framework across all hubs — no more reporting inconsistency.
  • Community governance approval: The community votes on the framework, budget, and conditions.
  • Post-mortem baseline: The 2025 post-mortem gives the 2026 program a documented foundation to build on — and be held against.
💬 Talking Point

"Every specific concern raised about 2025 has a specific remedy in 2026. Not vague promises — structural changes written into the contracts and the framework. Read the framework. If you find a gap we haven't addressed, tell us and we will address it. That's how this is supposed to work."
