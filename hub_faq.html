About the Committee

🏢 Membership & Community Committee

The MCC connects Intersect to the real world — supporting membership growth, regional onboarding, community programs, and ensuring Intersect membership translates into activity and impact.

Mission

The Membership & Community Committee (MCC) is an Intersect-facing Committee focused on reviewing and improving the membership offerings and membership experience, and growing and supporting participation in the Intersect community. MCC ensures that members are actively engaged by creating pathways for people and organizations to join.

This committee collaborates closely with the Intersect Steering Committee, as both committees have an Intersect-facing focus.

2026 Hub Framework

🚀 The 2026 Community Hub Model

A scalable, decentralized, performance-driven model — hubs request funding tied to measurable outputs rather than receiving fixed grants automatically.

Important: The MCC operates strictly as a coordination and evaluation body. It does not hold final decision-making authority over hub approvals, funding, or strategic direction. That authority rests with Intersect.

Philosophy

This framework promotes a flexible, community-driven hub model allowing hubs to evolve in response to regional needs rather than operate within rigid, predefined constraints. Rather than receiving fixed funding automatically, hubs request funding tied to measurable outputs — increasing accountability, rewarding ambition, and allowing hubs of all sizes to participate appropriately.

The approach reflects the varied capabilities demonstrated by existing hubs in 2024–2025, where hubs showed differing strengths, capacities, and modes of engagement. By empowering hubs to build on proven capabilities, the program delivers direct value: increasing governance participation, strengthening the DRep pipeline, enabling developer and institutional onboarding, and supporting the infrastructure required for global adoption.

Decision Process

5-Step Governance & Approval Flow

01
Application Stage

Public Hub Application

Formal hub application made public via Intersect with MCC assistance. Interested parties submit an Expression of Interest (EOI). Must include both hub proposal and hub manager application.

02
CHWG · ~1 Month

Community Hubs Application Working Group

A temporary working group (Intersect + MCC + volunteer hub managers + community members) evaluates applications and provides recommendations. It does not hold final decision-making power and dissolves once its purpose is fulfilled.

  • Evaluates & facilitates new hub applications
  • Functions as knowledge-sharing and resource coordination body
  • Promotes inclusivity — community members participate at every level
03
Final Authority

Intersect — Strategic Oversight & Approval

Final decision-making authority for hub approvals, funding, and strategic direction. CHWG and MCC submit a list of recommended hubs; Intersect ratifies and ensures strategic alignment with Cardano's broader governance vision.

04
Per-Event Funding

Request-Based Fund Distribution

Once appointed, hub managers request funding on a per-event basis. Every funding request must include:

  • Objective & related KPIs
  • Related milestones
  • Budget breakdown
  • Expected measurable outcome & timeline

Failure to deliver on expected outcomes results in lower priority for future funding requests and a more in-depth evaluation. Persistent inability to meet outcomes leads to appointment of a new hub manager.

05
Progression

Hub Tier Progression

Based on annual performance, hubs become eligible for tier upgrades that allow higher ranges of funding requests. Performance criteria include membership growth, engagement impact, and governance contributions.

Hub Progression

Every new hub starts at the same base level regardless of location. Progression to higher tiers is earned through demonstrated impact — not assumed.

Section 8.0 — Milestone Requirements

✅ Structured Milestone Framework

10 milestones across monthly, quarterly, and annual cycles. Designed to be flexible across hub tiers while ensuring consistent accountability and easy verification.

Monthly Milestones
Milestone 01
Community Activity Delivered
Hub must conduct a monthly virtual town hall AND complete at least one of:
  • Run an educational workshop
  • Organize a community event
Verification: links to town hall participation and event evidence.
Milestone 02
Growth Signals
Track and report on new membership numbers and event-to-member conversion rate. No growth requirement monthly — just tracking and reporting to demonstrate active management.
Milestone 03
Governance Touchpoints
At least one governance-related action:
  • Governance content published
  • Governance session hosted
  • Evidence of voter or DRep engagement effort
May be optional for Level 1 hubs
Quarterly Milestones
Milestone 04
Positive Community Health
Meet at least 2 of 3:
  • Positive membership growth or maintained retention threshold
  • Achieve minimum town hall attendance target
  • Event conversion rate within acceptable range
Milestone 05
Governance Advancements
Achieve one of:
  • Host 1 governance event
  • Increase DRep participation
  • Show measurable increase in voter participation
Ensures governance is not just symbolic.
Milestone 06
Educational Output
Achieve one (or two if possible):
  • Deliver workshops
  • Establish university partnership
  • Advance certification or internship pipeline
Annual Milestones — Renewal Checkpoints
Milestone 07
Sustainable Community Base
  • Positive annual membership growth or stable retention above threshold
  • Active leadership pipeline demonstrated
Long-term sustainability matters more than spikes.

Governance Impact
  • At least 1 governance event per quarter (aggregated annually)
  • AND either DRep growth OR 1 policy proposal influenced
Proves the hub contributes to decentralized governance.

  • Students trained or certifications completed
  • Internship placements made

  • Demonstrate measurable regional diversity growth, OR
  • Demonstrate deepened engagement within existing region

Community FAQ

💬 Addressing Questions About the 2025 Program

The 2026 framework is designed in direct response to the community's legitimate questions about 2025. These are honest, direct answers.

Financial Accountability
The Reality

The hub program had two distinct funding phases with different accountability structures — and this distinction matters a great deal.

  • Phase 1 (~6 months): Funded by Cardano Development Holdings (CDH), a private organization. These were private contracts — the same public disclosure standard that applies to treasury grants cannot be retroactively applied to private contracts.
  • Phase 2 (April–June 2025 bridge): Funded through public treasury withdrawal. These amounts are on-chain public record: see the table below.
  • All activity was milestone-gated: 48 Milestone Acceptance Forms (MAFs), DocuSign-verified, were required before any payment was released. Deliverables were confirmed before funds moved — in both phases.

The Reality

Japan's detailed cost breakdown is commendable. It's also worth being precise about what it represents: a self-reported Google Sheet from a hub that wound down operations and needed to account for funds being returned. The circumstance created the reporting — it was not a contract requirement.

All six hubs operated under the same CDH private contract structure, which did not mandate detailed financial disclosure. Holding five hubs to a standard that was never contractually required — and asking them to reconstruct 2-year-old records retroactively — is not a reasonable accountability mechanism. What we do have for all hubs: activity documentation, milestone sign-offs, event records, and membership outcomes.

Japan's level of financial reporting is exactly the standard the 2026 framework now requires of all hubs. The gap between Japan's reporting and the others was not evidence of wrongdoing — it was a structural omission in the original contract design. The 2026 framework corrects it: all hubs will operate under treasury funding with mandatory financial disclosure built into every contract from the start.

These are the Phase 2 bridge payment amounts — the treasury-funded period. The ADA amounts are publicly confirmed. Itemized receipts for those funds were not required by the contracts for this period. That is a gap in contract design, and it is one the 2026 framework directly addresses.

Japan's cost breakdown is the most useful public reference for what hub operations cost: approximately $19,000 USD across events (55%), staff labor (26%), hub management (18%), and tools (1%). The 2026 contracts require this level of itemized reporting by default from all hubs — that standard now applies program-wide from day one.

Program Value & ROI
The Reality

The post-mortem is the most complete picture of hub activity and outcomes ever compiled. The data is not perfect — but it is substantial and points in one consistent direction.

  • 70% of Intersect's individual members are located in hub regions. That is not coincidental.
  • Japan: 533 individual members from 61 events at ~$19K total spend ≈ ~$36 per individual member in direct event costs.
  • North America: 239 individual members, 225 associates, 6 enterprise members from 12 events.
  • These are not passive attendees — they made an active commitment to join and participate in Intersect governance.

Across all hubs, that adds up to 1,268 individual members, 1,111 associates, and 16 enterprise accounts — 70% of Intersect's total individual membership. Hubs create the kind of in-person trust that converts interested observers into committed members. Digital-only outreach has not demonstrated the ability to replicate that at the same depth or scale.

The Reality

The post-mortem report is the 2024–2025 record — the most comprehensive aggregation of hub activity, membership outcomes, and contract documentation ever compiled for this program.

If the concern is specifically about itemized expense receipts from private CDH-funded contracts — that standard cannot be retroactively applied to private contracting arrangements made under different terms. If the concern is about confidence that 2026 will have the accountability mechanisms 2025 lacked — the 2026 framework answers that directly. Delaying 2026 approval does not recover 2025 data. It forfeits the membership momentum and community relationships that hubs already built.

The Path Forward
Three Structural Differences
  • Funding source: 2026 hubs funded from community treasury — not private CDH funds. Treasury funding carries explicit public accountability requirements by default.
  • Reporting requirements: Standardized financial reporting and KPI disclosure are built into every 2026 contract from day one — not added retroactively.
  • Community governance approval: The community approves the framework, the budget, and the conditions. Accountability is not internal to Intersect — it is governed by the community itself.

The hubs program also now has something it lacked in 2025: a post-mortem. A documented record of what worked, what didn't, and what the program actually produced. The 2026 design is built on that foundation. Ultimately, the community approves the framework, the budget, and the conditions — that approval process is the accountability mechanism.

The Reality

"Mismanagement" implies behavioral failure. The gaps in 2025 were structural, not behavioral: reporting was inconsistent because reporting standards were not mandated. Financial disclosure was uneven because contracts did not require it uniformly. These are contract design problems, not character problems.

Evidence of actual delivery: 35 original contracts and 13 interim contracts completed with milestone sign-offs. 167 events run across six regions. 2,395 total Intersect members in hub regions. Six regions maintained active presence across a 14-month program period.

The people who ran 2025 hubs built the community relationships, local credibility, and institutional knowledge that a new program needs. The gaps in 2025 were in reporting requirements that were not written into the contracts — that is a design problem, not an execution problem. The 2026 framework fixes the design.
